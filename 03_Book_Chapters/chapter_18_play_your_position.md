## <span style="color: #0969DA">The Two Athletes</span>



It's late October. Junior varsity basketball tryouts just ended. Two sophomores walk out of the gym, same height, same build, same amount of sweat soaking through their shirts.

Meet Jordan and Casey. Both 6'1". Both put in the work over summer. Both dreaming of making varsity next year.

Fast forward one year.

**Jordan's Story:**

Jordan spends every practice trying to be like the players in NBA highlights. Watches Steph Curry drain three-pointers and tries to copy his form. Sees Ja Morant throw down dunks and attempts to do the same. Forces shots from logo range because that's what gets the crowd going.

The problem? Jordan's got decent handles but weak compared to the point guards. Can jump but not high enough to consistently finish at the rim. Shoots threes but only hits 28% of them.

Coach's feedback halfway through the season: "You're trying to do everything and excelling at nothing. What's your actual position?"

Jordan doesn't have an answer.

By season's end, Jordan's riding the bench most games. Stats: 3.2 points per game, mostly garbage time. Starts thinking maybe basketball isn't the right game.

**Casey's Story:**

Casey watches the same highlights but comes to a different conclusion: "I'll never be the tallest or most athletic player on the court."

So Casey asks a different question: "What am I actually good at?"

The honest answer? Vision. Passing. Defense. Not flashy stuff, but real skills.

Casey stops worrying about being the top scorer. Instead: becomes the best passer on the team. Leads the JV team in assists by December. Masters help-side defense, making life miserable for opposing scorers. Doesn't get the highlight reels, but makes everyone else better.

Coach's feedback: "You're irreplaceable because you play YOUR game, not someone else's."

By junior year, Casey's starting point guard. By senior year, team captain. The team wins the league title. Casey gets recruited by three colleges‚Äînot as a scorer, but as a floor general who makes everyone around them better.

Same talent. Same opportunity. Same gym.

Completely different results.

**The difference?**

Jordan tried to be good at everything and ended up mediocre at everything. Casey found a unique value and became exceptional at it.

Jordan competed in everyone else's game. Casey created their own.

Here's what nobody tells you: the key to winning isn't being the best at everything. It's being exceptional at your thing. The thing only you can do the way you do it.

Let's talk about how to find your position and dominate it.

## <span style="color: #0969DA">The Problem: The "Good at Everything" Trap</span>



There's this lie floating around that sounds really good on paper:

"Well-rounded is best. Be good at everything."

Your school pushes it. College applications reward it. Parents preach it. The message is clear: fill your resume with everything. Be the athlete AND the straight-A student AND the volunteer AND the club president AND the artist.

Don't specialize. Diversify.

Sounds smart, right?

Here's what actually happens when you try to be good at everything:

You spread yourself thin across ten different areas. You become mediocre at many things and exceptional at nothing. You're constantly comparing yourself to people who are better than you at their specialty, which means you always feel like you're losing.

You're exhausting yourself trying to compete on everyone else's terms. And in the process, you completely miss your actual competitive advantage.


> [!NOTE]
> **üí≠ Key Insight**
> *The Comparison Trap*


Social media makes this worse. You scroll through your feed and see everyone's highlight reel at their best thing.

Your friend posts about making honor roll. Another friend shares their new bench press PR. Someone else is performing in the school play. Another just launched a podcast.

And you're sitting there thinking: "I'm not the smartest. I'm not the strongest. I'm not the most talented. I'm not the most creative."

You compare your weak areas to their strengths. Result? You feel like you're losing at everything.

Reality check: you're not losing. You're just playing the wrong game.

Here's what the research shows. Economists call it "comparative advantage"‚Äîyou don't have to be the best at everything. You just need to focus on where you have a relative advantage.

Strengths-based psychology backs this up. Study after study shows that 80% of your growth comes from building on strengths, not fixing weaknesses. Yet most people spend 80% of their energy trying to fix what they're naturally bad at.

In business, they call it "differentiation strategy." You don't win by being the same as everyone but slightly better. You win by being different. By offering something nobody else can.

The pattern is clear across fields: specialists dominate. The well-rounded get replaced.

### <span style="color: #0969DA">The Solution: Own Your Position</span>

Whitney Wolfe Herd built a $13 billion company by refusing to play someone else's game.

After leaving Tinder, she could have tried to build a direct competitor. A dating app that was just like Tinder but a little better.

She didn't.

Instead, she created a completely different game. A women-first dating app where women make the first move. She turned what most people saw as a disadvantage‚Äîbeing a woman in male-dominated tech‚Äîinto her superpower.

She built Bumble on a simple principle: "Don't compete where you can't win. Create a game where your strengths are the winning moves."

That's what playing your position means.


> [!NOTE]
> **üí≠ Key Insight**
> *Here's how it works:*




1. Identify your unique strengths (what you're naturally good at)
2. Double down on those strengths (become exceptional, not just good)
3. Stop trying to compete where you're naturally disadvantaged
4. Turn your "weaknesses" or "differences" into competitive advantages
5. Create your own lane instead of fighting for space in someone else's

Think about it like an actual sports position.

In football, you don't ask your kicker to throw touchdowns. You don't ask your quarterback to kick field goals. Each position has a role. Each player maximizes their unique skills.

Life works the same way.

Notice: we're not asking "where can I be average at everything?" We're asking "where can I be exceptional at my thing?"

### <span style="color: #0969DA">Reframe: Strengths vs. Weaknesses</span>

Here's the old model most people follow:

"Fix your weaknesses until you're well-rounded."

Result? You become mediocre at everything, exceptional at nothing. You spend all your energy getting from terrible to okay instead of getting from good to great.

Here's the new model:

"Build on strengths. Manage around weaknesses."

Result? You become exceptional at your thing. You delegate, partner, or use tools to handle the rest.

This isn't giving up. This is strategy.


> [!NOTE]
> **üí≠ Key Insight**
> *The 80/20 Rule of Personal Development:*


80% of your growth comes from building strengths. 20% comes from fixing critical weaknesses‚Äîonly the ones that actually block you from progress.

If you're a writer who can't spell, use spellcheck. Don't spend three years becoming a spelling champion. That's not your position.

If you're a coder who struggles with public speaking, focus on writing clear documentation. You don't need to become a TED speaker. That's not your position.

If you're a visual artist who isn't great at math, use calculators and collaborate with someone who loves numbers. Don't torture yourself through advanced calculus. That's not your position.

Fix the weakness enough that it doesn't block you. Then get back to building your strength.

**Coach's Corner:**

> Well-rounded sounds safe. But in the real world, specialists win. Companies hire experts, not generalists. Audiences follow people who are the best at their thing, not pretty good at several things. Be spike-shaped‚Äîdeep expertise in your area‚Äînot flat across everything.

### <span style="color: #0969DA">Why This Works</span>

The research is clear on this.

Gallup studied this with their CliftonStrengths assessment. People who use their strengths daily are six times more engaged. Strengths-based teams outperform other teams by 12-15%. Focusing on strengths reduces burnout and increases performance.

Positive psychology pioneer Martin Seligman found the same pattern. Building on what you're good at creates sustainable excellence. Fixing weaknesses only gets you to "average."

In business strategy, Michael Porter proved that differentiation beats direct competition. A unique value proposition creates a defensible position. "Best" is subjective and constantly changing. "Different" is valuable and defensible.

The Japanese have a concept called "ikigai"‚Äîyour reason for being. It's the intersection of four things: what you're good at, what you love, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for.

Your position is where these overlap.

Whitney Wolfe Herd found hers. She didn't try to beat Tinder at their game‚Äîthe bro-culture, aggressive, male-dominated approach to dating apps. She created a different game entirely: women-first, safety-focused, respect-driven.

She turned being female in tech‚Äîoften seen as a disadvantage in Silicon Valley‚Äîinto her superpower. She built features that reflected her unique perspective. Women make the first move. BFF mode for friendships. Bizz mode for professional networking.

The result? A company worth $13 billion. She became the youngest woman to take a company public as CEO. All because she played her position instead of trying to be someone else.
