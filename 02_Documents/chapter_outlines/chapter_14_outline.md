## 1. Opening Story/Scenario (1-2 pages)

**The Two Friend Groups**

**Scene**: Two students with similar talent, different friend groups.

**Student A**:
- Hangs with friends who mock effort, celebrate mediocrity
- "Why are you trying so hard?" culture
- Friday night: mindless scrolling, complaining about school
- Peer pressure to skip practice, blow off studying
- One year later: same place, same habits, same results

**Student B**:
- Hangs with friends who celebrate growth, push each other
- "Let's get better together" culture
- Friday night: pickup basketball, studying together, building projects
- Peer encouragement to show up, level up
- One year later: varsity team, straight A's, leading a club

**The Uncomfortable Truth**: Student B's friends made them uncomfortable—but that discomfort was growth.

**Hook**: Same starting point. Same potential. Radically different outcomes. The only variable? Who they chose as teammates.

## 2. The Concept (3-4 pages)

### The Problem: Friendship by Default

Most people choose friends by:
- **Proximity**: "They're in my class"
- **Comfort**: "We've been friends since elementary"
- **Convenience**: "They live nearby"

Result: Friend groups formed by accident, not intention.

**What Happens**:
- Your peer group sets invisible standards
- You unconsciously match their effort level
- Their beliefs about what's possible become your beliefs
- Mediocrity becomes normalized

**The Science**: Mirror neurons, social contagion, peer effect research (Christakis & Fowler - behaviors spread through networks like viruses).

### The Solution: Strategic Friendship

**Not about being ruthless or fake**. It's about being intentional.

**The Kalanick Principle** (constructive lessons):
- Know what game you're playing (what do you want to achieve?)
- Identify who's playing at the level you aspire to
- Seek out people who make you uncomfortable (in a growth way)
- Build reciprocal value (be the friend you want to have)

**Three Types of Relationships**:

1. **Drainers** - Take energy, celebrate mediocrity, pull you down
2. **Maintainers** - Neutral, comfortable, keep you where you are
3. **Elevators** - Give energy, celebrate growth, push you higher

**Your job**: Minimize drainers, appreciate maintainers, maximize elevators.

### Why This Works

**Research Backing**:
- **Peer Effect Studies**: Students surrounded by high-achievers improve dramatically (Sacerdote, Dartmouth roommate studies)
- **Social Proof**: We look to peers to determine normal behavior
- **Accountability**: High-performers create positive peer pressure
- **Model Behavior**: You unconsciously adopt the habits of your peer group

**Jim Rohn Quote**: "You are the average of the five people you spend the most time with."

**The Math**:
- 5 friends averaging 6/10 effort → You trend toward 6/10
- 5 friends averaging 9/10 effort → You trend toward 9/10
- Same you. Different peer group. Different trajectory.

### The Output Focus

**What You'll Gain**:
- **Accelerated Growth** - Peers who push you level up faster
- **Higher Standards** - Your "normal" becomes exceptional
- **Better Opportunities** - High-performers attract opportunities
- **Sustained Motivation** - Peer energy keeps you going when discipline fades
- **Authentic Success** - Built with people who genuinely want you to win

**Reframe Table**:

| Don't Think | Think |
|-------------|-------|
| "I need to make friends" | "I'm building my championship team" |
| "I should be nice to everyone" | "I'm strategic about my inner circle" |
| "My friends are just for fun" | "My friends are my competitive advantage" |
| "I don't want to be alone" | "I'm selective about who gets my energy" |
| "Friendship happens naturally" | "I design my peer group intentionally" |

## 3. Real-World Examples (2-3 pages)

### Example 1: The Startup Founders (Contemporary)

**Mark Zuckerberg's Harvard Crew**:
- Surrounded himself with engineers who were better than him at specific things
- Dustin Moskovitz (operations), Eduardo Saverin (business), Chris Hughes (product)
- Created peer group that pushed everyone to level up
- **The Output**: Built Facebook because the team raised each other's game

**Lesson**: Even if you're talented, surround yourself with people who are better than you in specific areas.

### Example 2: The Championship Team (Sports)

**Steph Curry & Klay Thompson** (The "Splash Brothers"):
- Pushed each other in practice harder than games
- Created competitive friendship that elevated both
- "We make each other better" mentality
- Daily shooting competitions, film sessions together
- **The Output**: Multiple championships, revolutionized basketball

**Lesson**: The right teammate makes you better than you could be alone.

### Example 3: The Study Group (Relatable Student Example)

**High School Student - Maya**:
- Freshman year: friends who complained about homework, barely passed
- Sophomore year: joined study group with ambitious students
- New friends celebrated good grades, studied together
- Peer culture: "Let's ace this test" not "let's just pass"
- **The Output**: GPA went from 2.8 to 3.9, got into dream college

**Lesson**: Sometimes you need to change your peer group to change your trajectory.

## 4. Your Game Plan (2 pages)

### Step 1: Audit Your Current Team (Build Self-Awareness)

**The 5-Person Exercise**:

List the 5 people you spend the most time with:
1. _____________
2. _____________
3. _____________
4. _____________
5. _____________

For each person, ask:
- Do they push me to be better, or pull me toward comfortable?
- Do they celebrate my wins, or subtly diminish them?
- When I leave time with them, do I feel energized or drained?
- Are they playing the game I want to play?

**Categorize**: Drainer / Maintainer / Elevator

**The Output**: Crystal-clear picture of who's on your team right now.

### Step 2: Define Your Championship Team (Build Intentionality)

**Ask yourself**:
- What game am I playing? (What do I want to achieve?)
- What kind of people are playing at that level?
- What qualities do I need in my inner circle?

**Write Your Ideal Teammate Profile**:
- They're working on: _____________
- They value: _____________
- They challenge me to: _____________
- They celebrate: _____________

**The Output**: Clear criteria for who belongs in your inner circle.

### Step 3: Expand Your Elevator Circle (Build Your Team)

**Where to Find Elevators**:
- High-performing teams (sports, robotics, debate, theater)
- Competitive environments (honors classes, advanced programs)
- Skill-building groups (coding clubs, art studios, maker spaces)
- Ambitious projects (student government, startups, competitions)

**How to Build Relationships**:
1. **Show up consistently** - Be present where elevators hang out
2. **Add value first** - Help before asking for help
3. **Share your game** - Be honest about what you're working toward
4. **Create together** - Collaborate on projects
5. **Celebrate wins** - Be genuinely happy when they succeed

**The Output**: A growing network of people who raise your game.

### Step 4: Set Boundaries with Drainers (Protect Your Energy)

**This is the hardest part.** But necessary.

**Strategies**:
- **Gradual Distance**: Spend less time, not zero time (unless toxic)
- **Invite Them Up**: "Want to join me for this?" (Some will level up)
- **Set Clear Boundaries**: "I'm focusing on [goal] right now"
- **Be Kind but Firm**: You're not being mean—you're being intentional

**Script Examples**:
- "I can't hang out Friday—I'm training for [goal]"
- "I'm cutting back on [activity] to focus on [priority]"
- "I love you, but I need to spend time with people who are working toward [shared goal]"

**The Hard Truth**: Some friends won't understand. Some will judge you. Some will feel left behind. That's uncomfortable. But staying stuck with the wrong peer group is more uncomfortable in the long run.

**The Output**: Time and energy freed up for elevators.

### Step 5: Be the Elevator (Become the Teammate You Want)

**The Ultimate Move**: Don't just seek elevators—become one.

**How**:
- Celebrate others' wins genuinely
- Push friends to be their best (with love)
- Hold people accountable (without judgment)
- Share what you're learning
- Be the culture you want to be in

**The Reciprocity Principle**: The best way to attract high-performers is to be one.

**The Output**: You become magnetic to other elevators.

## 5. Practice Drills (1 page)

### Exercise 1: The 5-Person Audit

**Week 1 Challenge**: Track your time for 7 days. Who are you actually spending time with?

**Daily Log**:
- Monday: 2 hours with _______ (Drainer/Maintainer/Elevator)
- Tuesday: 3 hours with _______ (Drainer/Maintainer/Elevator)

**End of Week**: Calculate percentage of time with each category.

**Goal**: 60%+ of social time with Elevators within 90 days.

**Action This Week**:
- Reach out to 1 potential Elevator
- Offer value first (help, collaboration, genuine interest)
- Follow up consistently

**Practice saying no** to 3 invitations this week that don't align with your goals.

## 6. Post-Game Review (0.5 page)

**Reflection Questions**:

1. When I look at my current 5-person inner circle, am I proud of who I'm becoming?
2. What's one friendship that's been holding me back? Am I ready to create distance?
3. Who's one Elevator I admire? What's one way I can add value to them this week?
4. Am I being the kind of teammate I want to have? Where can I improve?
5. What's scarier: the discomfort of changing my peer group, or the regret of staying stuck?

**Key Takeaways**:
- Your peer group is your competitive advantage or your anchor
- Friendship by intention beats friendship by default
- Drainers/Maintainers/Elevators—spend 60%+ time with Elevators
- Be the Elevator you want to have
- Short-term discomfort (changing friend groups) → Long-term winning

**Next Level Preview**:
Now that you're building your peer team, Chapter 15 will show you how to find mentors—people 5-10 years ahead who've already won the game you're playing. Elevators are your lateral support. Mentors are your vertical advantage.
